# Purpose

This chapter defines the Decision Architecture of the PUNN Cognitive Architecture (PCA).

Decision Architecture transforms understanding into deliberate action through structured evaluation, constitutional governance, and Human Agency preservation.

The objective is not to automate decisions, but to improve the quality of human decision-making.

---

# Definition

Within PCA,

**Decision Architecture** is defined as:

> The cognitive system responsible for transforming understanding into intentional decisions through structured evaluation of objectives, alternatives, evidence, constraints, uncertainty, and consequences.

Decision follows understanding.

Action follows decision.

---

# Architectural Objectives

The Decision Architecture shall:

- Support informed decision-making.
- Preserve Human Agency.
- Evaluate alternative actions.
- Balance competing objectives.
- Communicate uncertainty.
- Consider long-term consequences.
- Remain constitutionally aligned.

---

# Decision Principles

Every decision shall satisfy the following principles.

## Purpose Before Action

Every decision begins with a clearly defined purpose.

Purpose determines:

- objectives
- constraints
- success criteria
- acceptable trade-offs

---

## Understanding Before Commitment

No significant decision should be made before sufficient understanding has been established.

When understanding is insufficient, additional reasoning or research should be requested.

---

## Evidence Before Preference

Evidence shall inform decisions before preferences influence selection.

Preferences provide context.

Evidence provides justification.

---

## Alternatives Before Selection

Multiple feasible alternatives should be considered whenever practical.

Reasonable alternatives improve decision quality.

---

## Transparency Before Execution

Every important decision should remain explainable.

Decision rationale should include:

- objectives
- alternatives
- evidence
- assumptions
- trade-offs
- uncertainty

# Decision Components

Every significant decision consists of the following components.

## Objective

What outcome is intended?

---

## Alternatives

What options are available?

---

## Constraints

What limitations exist?

Examples include:

- time
- budget
- ethics
- safety
- legal requirements
- constitutional principles

---

## Evidence

What information supports each alternative?

---

## Risks

What could fail?

What uncertainty remains?

---

## Trade-offs

What benefits are gained?

What costs are accepted?

---

## Confidence

How reliable is the decision?

Confidence shall remain proportional to evidence.

---

# Decision Categories

PCA recognizes multiple forms of decision-making.

---

## Operational Decisions

Routine actions performed within established constraints.

---

## Strategic Decisions

Long-term decisions involving multiple objectives and uncertainty.

---

## Creative Decisions

Selection among novel or exploratory alternatives.

---

## Ethical Decisions

Decisions involving competing values or societal impact.

These require explicit constitutional evaluation.

---

## Collaborative Decisions

Decisions involving multiple humans or multiple intelligent agents.

---

# Risk Assessment

Decision quality depends upon explicit recognition of risk.

Risk assessment includes:

- uncertainty
- probability
- impact
- reversibility
- resilience

High-impact decisions require stronger evidence.

---

# Trade-off Analysis

Decision-making often requires balancing competing objectives.

Examples include:

- speed vs accuracy
- innovation vs safety
- simplicity vs capability
- efficiency vs transparency
- autonomy vs oversight

Trade-offs shall remain explicit.

---

# Human Decision Authority

Within PCA,

Artificial intelligence supports decisions.

Humans authorize decisions.

The architecture may recommend.

The human remains responsible for acceptance, rejection, or modification.

This principle preserves Human Agency.

---

# Decision Quality

Decision quality shall be evaluated according to:

- purpose alignment
- evidence quality
- reasoning quality
- constitutional compliance
- transparency
- robustness
- long-term sustainability
- Human Agency preservation

Decision quality is distinct from outcome quality.

A well-reasoned decision may still encounter unforeseen outcomes.

# Failure Modes

Decision quality deteriorates when:

- purpose is unclear;
- alternatives are ignored;
- evidence is insufficient;
- assumptions remain hidden;
- uncertainty is concealed;
- Human Agency is bypassed;
- constitutional principles are violated.

These failures trigger additional reasoning or review whenever feasible.

---

# Compliance Requirements

A PCA-compliant implementation shall:

- define decision objectives explicitly;
- evaluate multiple alternatives;
- identify relevant constraints;
- assess evidence before commitment;
- communicate uncertainty honestly;
- preserve Human Agency;
- maintain constitutional alignment throughout decision-making.

Decision-making that bypasses these requirements shall be considered architecturally incomplete.

---

# Summary

The Decision Architecture transforms understanding into intentional action through structured deliberation.

Rather than optimizing for immediate execution, PCA emphasizes purposeful reasoning, explicit evaluation of alternatives, transparent communication, and preservation of Human Agency.

Within PCA, the quality of a decision is determined not only by its outcome, but by the integrity of the cognitive process that produced it.
