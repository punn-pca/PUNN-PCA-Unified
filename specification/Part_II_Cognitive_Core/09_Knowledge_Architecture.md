# Purpose

This chapter defines the Knowledge Architecture of the PUNN Cognitive Architecture (PCA).

Knowledge Architecture specifies how knowledge is represented, organized, maintained, and utilized throughout the cognitive lifecycle.

Its purpose is not merely to store information, but to support reasoning, learning, explanation, and decision-making through structured representations of reality.

---

# Definition

Within PCA,

**Knowledge Architecture** is defined as:

> The structured organization of concepts, relationships, principles, mental models, and contextual information that enables intelligent reasoning and continual understanding.

Knowledge exists to improve cognition.

It is not an end in itself.

---

# Architectural Principles

The Knowledge Architecture shall satisfy the following principles.

- Knowledge is structured rather than accumulated.
- Relationships are as important as individual facts.
- Knowledge supports reasoning.
- Knowledge evolves through learning.
- Knowledge remains explainable.
- Knowledge preserves uncertainty when appropriate.

---

# Knowledge Hierarchy

Knowledge is organized into multiple levels.

## Level 1 — Observations

Raw information collected from reality.

Examples include:

- measurements
- events
- documents
- conversations
- sensor data

Observations are descriptive rather than explanatory.

---

## Level 2 — Facts

Validated observations accepted as reliable within a defined context.

Facts remain open to revision when stronger evidence becomes available.

---

## Level 3 — Concepts

Abstract representations of entities, properties, behaviors, or phenomena.

Concepts organize facts into meaningful categories.

---

## Level 4 — Relationships

Connections between concepts.

Relationships include:

- causation
- dependency
- hierarchy
- similarity
- contradiction
- association

Relationships provide structure for reasoning.

---

## Level 5 — Principles

General rules describing consistent patterns across multiple observations.

Principles support prediction and explanation.

---

## Level 6 — Mental Models

Integrated structures explaining how systems operate.

Mental models combine:

- concepts
- relationships
- principles
- assumptions
- evidence

Mental models are the primary units of understanding within PCA.

---

# Knowledge Representation

Knowledge may be represented through multiple complementary forms.

Examples include:

- semantic networks
- ontologies
- knowledge graphs
- symbolic representations
- probabilistic models
- vector representations
- hybrid structures

PCA does not mandate a single representation.

Representations shall preserve architectural semantics rather than implementation details.

---

# Knowledge Categories

The architecture distinguishes several categories of knowledge.

---

## Declarative Knowledge

Knowledge describing what is true.

Examples include:

- definitions
- facts
- theories
- concepts

---

## Procedural Knowledge

Knowledge describing how tasks are performed.

Examples include:

- algorithms
- workflows
- procedures
- reasoning strategies

---

## Contextual Knowledge

Knowledge describing when and where information applies.

Context determines the validity of many conclusions.

---

## Experiential Knowledge

Knowledge derived from reflection, learning, and accumulated experience.

This knowledge continuously evolves.

---

## Institutional Knowledge

Shared knowledge maintained across organizations, projects, or multi-agent systems.

Institutional knowledge persists independently of individual participants.

---

# Knowledge Lifecycle

Knowledge evolves continuously.

The lifecycle consists of:

Observation

↓

Validation

↓

Representation

↓

Integration

↓

Reasoning

↓

Evaluation

↓

Revision

↓

Reuse

↓

Learning

Knowledge remains dynamic throughout this process.

---

# Knowledge Integrity

Knowledge integrity requires explicit distinction between:

- observation
- fact
- inference
- assumption
- hypothesis
- opinion
- uncertainty

Mixing these categories reduces reasoning quality and architectural reliability.

---

# Knowledge Quality

Knowledge quality shall be evaluated according to:

Accuracy

Consistency

Completeness

Relevance

Timeliness

Traceability

Explainability

Adaptability

No single metric is sufficient.

---

# Knowledge Traceability

Every significant knowledge element should be traceable to its origin.

Traceability may include:

- source
- evidence
- author
- timestamp
- revision history
- confidence

Traceability supports transparency and future evaluation.

---

# Knowledge Evolution

Knowledge is expected to change.

Evolution may occur through:

- new observations
- improved evidence
- corrected reasoning
- scientific discovery
- reflection
- criticism

Knowledge revision strengthens the architecture.

It does not weaken it.

---

# Interaction with Cognitive DNA

Knowledge Architecture supports every stage of the Cognitive DNA.

Observation

provides new knowledge.

Memory

retrieves relevant knowledge.

Mental Models

organize knowledge.

Reasoning

operates upon knowledge.

Critique

evaluates knowledge.

Learning

updates knowledge.

Knowledge therefore serves as the informational foundation for the complete cognitive lifecycle.

---

# Architectural Responsibilities

The Knowledge Architecture shall:

- organize information coherently;
- preserve semantic relationships;
- support transparent reasoning;
- enable continual learning;
- facilitate knowledge reuse;
- maintain epistemic integrity;
- evolve through evidence.

---

# Compliance Requirements

A PCA-compliant implementation shall:

- distinguish knowledge from data;
- preserve semantic relationships;
- support structured mental models;
- maintain knowledge traceability;
- represent uncertainty explicitly;
- permit continual knowledge revision.

Implementations may differ internally but shall preserve these architectural properties.

---

# Summary

Knowledge Architecture transforms isolated information into structured understanding.

By organizing concepts, relationships, principles, and mental models into a coherent semantic framework, PCA enables reasoning systems to explain reality, support decision-making, and continuously improve through learning.

Knowledge is therefore not merely stored within PCA.

It is continuously constructed, evaluated, refined, and applied throughout the cognitive lifecycle.
